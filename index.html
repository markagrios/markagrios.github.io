About me.

Hey! I'm Mark! I'm a senior in high school interested in everything I don't already know. Most of my curiousity lies within the realm of sciences and math rather than the arts but I love it all just the same. I've been involved in my high school's TSA (Technology Students Association) chapter for four years and was vice president my junior year. I have also spent my four years of high school on the robotics team being co-captain the last three years. In middle school at around the time I learned to ride a unicycle, I picked up the art of performing magic. Since then I have explored and created many of my own illusions and have performed on the streets of Washington D.C. doing tricks with everyday items and hypnotizing spectators. I've always been interested in music. I appreciate the structure and complexities that lie within a piece of music and I like taking apart movements and see what makes certain parts so enjoyable. The only visual art media I am somewhat involved in is photography. I enjoy exploring with light and contrast so black and white prints (especially of nature) I often find intriguing. However, more than music and photography, I find language the most amazing. With my practice in the art of hypnotism, I have come to appreciate the small facets of meaning we can put into spoken language. The deep ambiguity of speech only seems ambiguous because of the lack of conscious thought we put into it. I find this truly astounding.

People's distaste with Mathematics.

Why do some people hate math?

People love learning. Some people will say they don't because they associate it with school but people do indeed love to learn. A majority of what people enjoy learning is the world around them and how it works. Mathematics is a tool. No one appreciates tools. People will look at the very first airplane and find it truly amazing, man conquering the ancient dream of flight. How many people stop and think about what tools the Wright brothers used to construct their flying machine?

People don't like being told what to do. Many will say rules are meant to be broken. It is driven too deep into people's head that you can never break the rules of math. This is the biggest deal breaker for people learning mathematics. People go through school learning about the controversial art movements of Monet or the disputed works of Voltaire. People learn how often change must take place in order for any progress to be made. People also learn how you must always do math this very specific way if you want the right answers. We are not taught, however, how much mathematics has changed since its beginnings. The ancient Greeks believed only in rational numbers, number that can be expressed as a ratio of two integers (-1/2, 10, 54/11). The square root of 2 is not rational. It is very easy to constuct, take to lines of length 1 and form a right angle, the length between the two endpoints not touching is the square root of 2. Similarly, the square root of -1 is a bit harder to grasp. Take a number line from negative infinity to positive infinity, any "real" number will be on that line, I could have 2 eggs, the ratio of the circle's perimeter to its diameter is pi which is to the right of 3, I can be $1000 in debt which is far to the left of 0. The square root of -1 will not be on that line. When people discovered these "numbers that can't exist" the threw them out, they said "we can't use these, they make no sense." Imagine going through art school painting pictures of fruit, and one day you decided to paint a picture of your friend deeply focused on his painting of fruit. Your first question when your teacher tells you "all of art is about painting fruit" would be "why?" People looked up at the sky and saw birds and thought that man should someday fly. Lots of the world said "no, man doesn't fly, birds fly, but not man."

Every concept has rules. If I drop this bowl of noodles, it will fall and smash. Gravity is relatively easy so grasp. However, rules only apply to what follows them. This is probably the most counter-intuitive idea I've discovered, it's bizarrely backwards. There is no universal space called "rules" that everything in the universe fits in; there are pockets of rules that each have a set of things they apply to and each pocket of rules is different. A simple rule in mathematics is that if you square a number you will always have a positive result. When the idea of the number 'i' was accepted the idea that it didn't play by that rule was also accepted.

When people say the hate math, most of the time they are saying the hate all the rules math has. They are not taught the beauty math has similar to art and music. The philosophy of math lies deep within the practice rather than the outcome. Most other fields' true elegance is contained in the finished product, a six voice fugue, an impressionist landscape, a flying machine that actually flies. The study of mathematics appreciates finding connections. Somehow linking these two seemingly unrelated ideas is a victory in this realm.
